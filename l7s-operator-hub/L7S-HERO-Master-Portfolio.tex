\begin{goldbox}{1\quad The Role of Escrow}
A neutral third party holds funds and documents until every agreed-upon
condition is satisfied. Neither buyer nor seller can move the asset
unilaterally — the escrow agent releases only when the contract's
milestones are independently verified. This removes counterparty risk
from high-value transfers and creates a clean, auditable chain of custody.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Buyer protection:} funds are not released until deliverables clear.
  \item \textbf{Seller protection:} proof-of-funds is locked before work begins.
  \item \textbf{Auditability:} every release is timestamped and condition-gated.
\end{itemize}
\end{goldbox}

\begin{goldbox}{2\quad Multi-Sig Security Protocol}
Multi-signature ("multi-sig") authorization requires \emph{M-of-N}
approvals before a release executes. A 2-of-3 arrangement is typical:
the buyer, the seller, and the escrow agent each hold one key, and any
two must co-sign. No single party — not even the agent — can move funds alone.
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{>{\bfseries}l l}
\rowcolor{L7Sgoldsoft} Signer & Key Role \\
Party A (Buyer)  & Authorizes against deliverables met \\
Party B (Seller) & Authorizes against funds confirmed \\
Escrow Agent     & Breaks ties / enforces contract conditions \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\textit{Release rule: any 2 of 3 keys = transaction settles.}
\end{goldbox}

\begin{goldbox}{3\quad The Trust Logic}
Trust is engineered, not assumed. Escrow supplies the neutral custodian;
multi-sig distributes authority so collusion requires a majority. Together
they convert a fragile two-party promise into a verifiable, tamper-evident
settlement system — the foundation of every L7S HERO premium transaction.
\end{goldbox}
